18 Key Takeaways

1
AI revenue went from unquantified to $20 billion in 3 years. The single most important transformation in Broadcom’s history: from $2B in compute offload ASICs (FY2022) to $20B in AI semiconductor revenue (FY2025), a 10x expansion that redefined the company’s identity and valuation.
2
XPU customer diversification de-risked the AI thesis. The expansion from 1 customer (Google TPU) to 5 production customers plus 2 prospects eliminated the single-customer concentration risk that plagued the stock through FY2023. Each customer conversion (especially the 4th with $10B+ orders) represented a step-function increase in addressable demand.
3
VMware integration executed at unprecedented speed. Operating margins went from <30% to 70% in under 12 months — the fastest margin expansion in enterprise software M&A history. The forced VCF subscription transition, while initially controversial, created a high-visibility recurring revenue stream.
4
FY2024 AI guidance was raised 4 consecutive quarters. From an implied ~$7.5B (>25% of semi) at initial guidance to $12.2B actual — a 63% increase from initial outlook. This pattern of systematic under-promise and over-deliver became a hallmark of Broadcom’s AI disclosure strategy.
5
EBITDA margins traced a V-shape from 65% → 60% → 67%. The VMware-induced margin dip was fully recovered within 8 quarters, with margins reaching all-time highs by FQ3 2025. The combination of high-margin software mix and operating leverage from AI scale drove the recovery far faster than expected.
6
The $60–90B FY2027 AI SAM was never revised. Introduced in FQ4 2024 and reaffirmed for 5 consecutive quarters, this long-term framework provided a credible anchor for AI growth expectations. The $73B AI backlog disclosed in FQ4 2025 made the midpoint look increasingly conservative.
7
Ethernet won the AI networking war by default. Broadcom’s claim that 7 of 8 largest AI clusters use its Ethernet technology (FQ2 2024) settled the Ethernet vs. InfiniBand debate for scale-out. The launch of SUE (<250ns latency) in FQ3 2025 opened a second front in scale-up, directly challenging NVLink.
8
Non-AI semiconductors experienced a muted U-shaped recovery. Unlike prior cycles with sharp V-recoveries, the non-AI business bottomed at $17.8B (FY2024) and recovered slowly through FY2025. The muted recovery reflected enterprise caution and capital reallocation toward AI, with broadband as the only genuine bright spot.
9
Gross margins rose despite AI XPU dilution. AI XPUs include HBM memory that dilutes semiconductor gross margins, yet consolidated GM rose from 74–76% to 77–79% because the growing VMware software contribution (~90%+ GM) more than offset the semiconductor mix headwind.
10
$8.8B in debt paydown while returning $39.7B to shareholders. Broadcom managed to aggressively deleverage from $75.9B peak debt to $67.1B while simultaneously returning $22.2B (FY2024) and $17.5B (FY2025) to shareholders. The elimination of all floating-rate debt was a key risk-reduction milestone.
11
Networking technology roadmap advanced through 3 generations. Tomahawk 4 → TH5 (51.2 Tbps) → TH6 (102.4 Tbps), plus the new Jericho4 and silicon photonics, positioned Broadcom at the center of AI data center infrastructure. Each generation roughly doubled bandwidth, maintaining the cadence needed to support AI cluster scaling.
12
Revenue nearly doubled in 3 years: $33.2B → $63.9B. This 93% growth was split roughly equally between the two transformative vectors: VMware adding ~$20B in software and AI semiconductors growing by ~$18B. Entering FY2026 at a ~$76B+ annualized run-rate, Broadcom is positioned among the largest technology companies globally.
