Written Analysis: Broadcom's Intermittent Disclosure Patterns

1. Three Simultaneous Transformations

Broadcom's intermittent KPIs reveal a company undergoing three simultaneous transformations, each with its own metric lifecycle:

Transformation 1: AI Revenue Emergence. The metrics evolved from "compute offload ASICs" (FQ1 2022) to "AI Revenue" (FQ2 2023) to "AI SAM" (FQ4 2024) to "AI Backlog" (FQ3 2025) to "FY2027 Revenue Line of Sight" (FQ1 2026). Each metric matured from speculative to concrete as the business scaled. The customer count journey from 1 to 6 (Sections 1-2) is the clearest expression of this arc.

Transformation 2: VMware Integration. ABV, VCF adoption, operating cost run-rate, and restructuring charges all followed a classic acquisition integration lifecycle: intensive disclosure during the restructuring (5-6 quarters), then retirement as the business normalized. The entire VMware integration metric suite had a ~6-quarter active life before transitioning to TCV and broader software metrics.

Transformation 3: Balance Sheet Restructuring. The floating-to-fixed debt transition (Section 9) was a pure execution story — $30B eliminated in 7 quarters — tracked with the precision of a project management dashboard. Once complete, the metrics disappeared.

2. The Metric Succession Pattern

Broadcom exhibits a distinctive pattern: old metrics are retired and replaced by new ones that better capture the business reality. Semiconductor end-market breakdowns were replaced by AI/Non-AI. ABV was replaced by TCV. SAM was replaced by backlog. Renewal rates were replaced by VCF adoption. Each replacement signals a strategic pivot: the company is telling investors "stop thinking about us this way; start thinking about us that way."

Hock Tan's explicit retirement of the SAM metric ("stop talking about SAM now") in FQ2 2025 is the clearest example. SAM was theoretical; backlog is contractual. The shift from theoretical to contractual framing mirrors Broadcom's broader culture of under-promising and over-delivering.

3. The Legacy Business Disappearance

Perhaps the most striking pattern is what stopped being disclosed: the 5-segment semiconductor breakdown (Section 4), the pre-VMware renewal rates (Section 8), and the hardware backlog/lead times (Section 10) all disappeared as their relevance faded. Broadband went from a $1.2B/quarter business to $465M — a 61% decline that management simply stopped drawing attention to once AI revenue dwarfed it. Industrial revenue at $173M/quarter became rounding error on a $14B semiconductor segment.

This selective disclosure disappearance is not deceptive; it reflects genuine strategic prioritization. The legacy segments collectively represent <25% of semiconductor revenue and are shrinking, while AI represents >60% and is growing 70%+ annually. Investors following the old framework would miss the real story.

4. The XPU Customer Count as Master Narrative

If there is one intermittent metric that captures the entire Broadcom AI story, it is the XPU customer count (Section 1). Each customer addition — from 1 (Google, FQ2 2023) to 2 (FQ1 2024) to 3 (FQ3 2024) to 6 (FQ1 2026) — was a revenue step-function. The progression from anonymous "hyperscalers" to explicit naming (FQ4 2025-FQ1 2026) reflects both the maturation of these relationships and the competitive dynamics that made secrecy less necessary (NVIDIA's dominance meant custom silicon adoption was no longer a competitive surprise).

5. Forward-Looking Metric Hierarchy

For forward analysis, the most valuable intermittent metrics are: (1) AI backlog ($73B, providing 18-month visibility); (2) XPU customer count and prospect pipeline (the S-curve of adoption); and (3) Total Contract Value in software ($10.4B, indicating subscription durability). These three metrics collectively determine Broadcom's revenue trajectory for the next 2-3 years and are not available in any standardized financial filing — only in earnings call commentary.
