FX as margin tailwind/headwind: TSMC’s guidance sensitivity is approximately 40–50 bps of gross margin per 1 TWD/USD move. When actual FX rates came in weaker than assumptions (as in H2 2022), TSMC captured a significant GM tailwind that partly explains the systematic GM beats. Full-year pattern: In 3 of 4 completed fiscal years (2022, 2024, 2025), TSMC raised its full-year revenue guidance through the year and actual growth exceeded even the raised targets. Only FY2023 saw guidance cuts, as the inventory correction proved deeper than initially expected. CapEx guidance tends to be lowered or tightened during downturns (2022–2023) and narrowed upward during upcycles (2024–2025), tracking management’s confidence in demand visibility.

6 Key Takeaways

1
TSMC never misses the low end. In 15 quarters of guidance, actual revenue always came in at or above the bottom of the range. This reflects management’s strong 90-day demand visibility and deliberate conservatism in setting guidance floors.
2
Gross margin guidance is the most conservative metric. 15/15 quarters beat the GM midpoint, with an average surprise of +155 bps. This systematic upside appears structural rather than accidental — management consistently incorporates buffers for cost improvement, FX moves, and utilization optimization.
3
Operating margin amplifies the beat through operating leverage. OM beat its midpoint 15/15 quarters with an even wider +231 bps average surprise. The differential vs GM (+76 bps) comes from fixed-cost leverage: when revenue exceeds guidance, OpEx as a percentage of revenue naturally declines.
4
Revenue beats accelerated with the AI cycle. The average revenue beat vs midpoint was +0.3% during 2022–2023 (inventory correction) vs +3.0% during 2024–2025 (AI demand surge). AI demand appears harder for TSMC to forecast, or management adopted wider conservatism buffers as visibility improved.
5
The Q2 2025 revenue beat (+4.5% vs mid) was the largest single-quarter surprise at $1.3B above the midpoint. This coincided with the acceleration of AI accelerator chip demand and likely reflected upside from CoWoS advanced packaging capacity coming online faster than expected.
6
Only two revenue misses vs midpoint: Q4 2022 and Q1 2023. Both occurred during the post-pandemic inventory correction when smartphone and PC demand deteriorated faster than TSMC’s guidance cycle. Critically, even these misses stayed within the guidance range — highlighting the conservatism of the low-end floor.
7
Full-year guidance is systematically conservative. In 3 of 4 years (2022, 2024, 2025), TSMC raised full-year revenue guidance intra-year and actual growth still exceeded the final raised number. Initial guidance appears to be set at a level management is confident of exceeding.
8
Q3 2024 was the inflection quarter. The +330 bps GM beat and +400 bps OM beat that quarter marked the moment AI demand overwhelmed the drag from N3 ramp dilution. From Q3 2024 onward, beat magnitudes structurally widened across all three metrics.
9
FX assumptions add a hidden buffer. Management sets FX assumptions conservatively. When actual TWD/USD rates came in weaker than assumed (common in 2022), the resulting margin tailwind contributed to GM beats. Even in Q3 2025 when the TWD dramatically strengthened (assumption: 29.0), TSMC still beat by +300 bps on GM, proving pricing power dominates FX effects.
10
CapEx guidance is the one metric where TSMC undershoots. In both 2022 ($36.3B vs $40–44B guidance) and 2023 ($30.5B vs $32–36B), actual CapEx came in below the guided range. Management appears to set CapEx guidance optimistically relative to spending pace, then adjusts down if demand softens.
11
The Q1 2026 guidance ($34.6–35.8B, GM 63–65%) would be a new high-water mark on both revenue and GM if achieved. The guidance midpoint of $35.2B represents +4.5% QoQ and the 64% GM midpoint would be 170 bps above Q4 2025’s already-record 62.3%. Based on the scorecard pattern, an upside surprise is statistically likely.
12
Investors should “model the beat.” TSMC’s systematic guidance conservatism means the midpoint is effectively a floor, not a central estimate. Over 15 quarters, the high end of revenue guidance has been a better predictor of actuals than the midpoint, and for margins, even the high end understates reality two-thirds of the time.
