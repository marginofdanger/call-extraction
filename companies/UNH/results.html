Systematically underestimating medical costs. UNH missed its initial MCR guidance in all 3 years it was explicitly provided, with misses accelerating from +60 bps (FY 2023) to +160 bps (FY 2024) to +260 bps (FY 2025). The FY 2025 miss was staggering: initial guidance of 86.5% actual of 89.1% reflects $6.5B in excess medical costs driven by a 2× acceleration in Medicare care activity, provider coding intensity, and specialty drug adoption. By contrast, once guidance was revised, the final MCR landed within 15 bps of the updated midpoint in all 3 years—indicating management can estimate near-term trends but consistently underestimates the pace of medical cost escalation at the start of each year.

Revenue growth resilient even as earnings collapsed. Revenue grew from $324B to $448B (+38%) over 4 years while adjusted EPS fell 26% ($22.19 to $16.35). This disconnect reflects the structural compression: medical costs grew faster than premiums (MCR expanded 710 bps from 82.0% to 89.1%), and the $3.1B cyberattack plus $1.6B restructuring charge consumed operating earnings. Cash flow deteriorated sharply: CFO/net income fell from 1.3× (FY 2022–2023) to ~1.5× in FY 2025 on a much lower net income base, and guided CFO fell from ~$33B to ~$16B before actual came in at $19.7B.

Q1 Update (Apr ’25) Cut EPS: $26.00–$26.50 (−12%)  |  MCR: 87.5% ±50 bps  |  Revenue: $450–$455B
Q2 Update (Jul ’25) Cut EPS: ≥$16 (−39%)  |  MCR: 89.25% ±25 bps  |  Revenue: ~$448B  |  CFO: ~$16B  |  New CEO

Verdict EPS fell 45% from initial midpoint. Worst guidance miss in company history. Two cuts totaling −$13.40/share. MCR 260 bps above initial guide. CFO of $19.7B exceeded revised $16B guide, partly from working capital timing.

5 Key Takeaways

1
A tale of two eras. FY 2022–2023 saw 6 consecutive guidance raises, with EPS beating the final high end both years. FY 2024–2025 reversed completely: 3 guidance cuts, a $3.1B cyberattack, and the largest EPS collapse in company history (−45% vs initial FY 2025 guidance).
2
Medical costs were chronically underestimated. MCR exceeded initial guidance midpoint in all 3 years it was explicitly guided, with misses accelerating: +60 bps (FY 2023), +160 bps (FY 2024), +260 bps (FY 2025). The cumulative miss reflects a structural failure to anticipate Medicare care activity acceleration, provider coding intensity, and specialty drug adoption.
3
Revenue growth masked earnings deterioration. Revenue compounded at 11.3% annually ($324B to $448B), yet adjusted EPS fell 26% ($22.19 to $16.35) over the same period. The divergence was driven entirely by MCR expansion: medical costs grew faster than premium revenue in every year from FY 2023 onward.
4
FY 2025 was the worst guidance year in UNH history. Initial EPS of $29.50–$30.00 was cut twice to “at least $16,” a 46% reduction from the initial midpoint. The $13.40 miss vs initial midpoint dwarfs any prior variance. Drivers: $6.5B excess medical costs, $1.6B restructuring charge, leadership change, and portfolio exits.
5
Cash flow provided a buffer in the down years. CFO exceeded the revised guide in both FY 2024 (met $24B) and FY 2025 ($19.7B vs $16B guide, +23%). Even in the worst year, cash conversion remained above 1.0× net income. However, CFO fell from $29.1B (FY 2023) to $19.7B (FY 2025)—a 32% decline.
6
The cyberattack was a $3.1B event with lasting effects. The February 2024 Change Healthcare attack produced $2.2B in direct response costs and $867M in business disruption, yet management held EPS guidance at $27.50–$28.00 through Q2 before narrowing by just $0.25 in Q3. The cash flow impact (~$4B timing shift) recovered in FY 2025.
7
V28 created an $11B headwind at OptumHealth. The CMS V28 risk model phase-in (2024–2026) generated $11B in cumulative headwind for OptumHealth—$2B and $1B more than initially estimated. Through FY 2025, $7B was realized with $4B remaining for 2026, only ~50% expected to be offset through pricing and benefit changes.
8
OptumHealth VBC margins collapsed. Value-based care margins fell from ~5% (FY 2023) to ~1% (FY 2025), driven by V28 risk model impacts, negative-margin new patient cohorts, and network overexpansion. The 2024–2025 cohorts (40% of patients) ran at negative margins. Long-term target of 6%–8% was revised down from the original range, with recovery now expected in 2027+.
9
Once revised, guidance was accurate. In FY 2022–2023, final guidance midpoints missed actuals by only $0.22 on average. In FY 2024, the narrowed $27.50–$27.75 range captured the $27.66 actual precisely. In FY 2025, the “at least $16” floor was cleared by just $0.35. Management can estimate near-term trajectory but consistently fails to anticipate the pace of structural deterioration at the start of each year.
10
FY 2026 is a reset, not a recovery. EPS guidance of >$17.75 implies only +8.6% growth from the depressed $16.35 base—still 40% below the FY 2022 peak of $22.19. Revenue is guided down for the first time (~$440B vs $448B) as UHC sheds 2.3M–2.8M members. Management frames 2026 as a “stepping off point” for a return to 13%–16% long-term EPS growth, but that target now looks aspirational given 3 consecutive years of margin compression.
11
The long-term growth target was never formally lowered. The 13%–16% EPS CAGR target was reaffirmed in every Q4 call from FY 2022 through FY 2025, even as actual EPS compounded at −10% over 3 years (FY 2022–2025). Management consistently described it as the “long-term potential” while acknowledging near-term headwinds. Credibility of this target depends entirely on whether the 2026–2027 recovery materializes.
12
Medicare funding cuts compounded the problem. CMS imposed ~$130B in cumulative Medicare funding reductions over 3 years (2024–2026), including V28 risk model changes (>$6B enterprise headwind) and inadequate rate updates. The 2027 Advance Notice was described as “disappointing” and will likely require further benefit reductions and geographic exits—extending the margin recovery timeline.
