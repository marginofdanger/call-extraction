What the Disclosure Patterns Reveal

The V28 Cascade: How a Rate Cut Became a Multi-Front Crisis

The CMS V28 risk adjustment model change, initially described with measured optimism in Q1 2023, proved to be the catalyst for a cascading series of crises. The direct funding impact — quantified at $11B over three years for OptumHealth alone — was compounded by second-order effects: competitor plan exits that dumped disengaged patients into Optum's VBC network, premium increases for group MA retirees that changed utilization behavior, and a broader industry repricing dynamic that exposed the fragility of aggressively priced benefit designs. By Q4 2025, management was invoking "$130B in cumulative funding reductions" to signal systemic risk to the entire Medicare Advantage program.

Growth at the Expense of Margins: The OptumHealth Story

OptumHealth's trajectory illustrates the tension between growth and profitability in value-based care. Patient count nearly tripled from ~1.8M to 4.7M between 2022 and 2024, but the VBC operating margin collapsed from ~5% to under 1% over the same period. The cohort-level data disclosed in Q2 2025 was revelatory: mature (pre-2021) cohorts earned 8%+ margins, but 2024–2025 cohorts — representing 40% of all patients — operated at "negative margins near double digits." The long-term margin target was revised downward twice, from 8%–10% to 7.7%–8.0% to 6%–8%, each revision acknowledging that the maturation timeline for new patient cohorts was longer and more costly than initially anticipated.

The Cyberattack as Inflection Point

The February 2024 Change Healthcare cyberattack functioned as more than a one-time cost event. Its $3.09B direct impact in 2024 (plus ~$800M in 2025 write-downs) was substantial, but the second-order effects were arguably more damaging: suspended care management activities contributed ~40 bps to MCR, provider level-of-care waivers triggered a coding intensity upshift that persisted long after the waivers were rescinded, and customers diversified away from Change's clearinghouse platform. The backlog declined from $35B to $32.1B, and the competitive position of the clearinghouse business was permanently impaired. The cyberattack also coincided with and amplified several other pressures, making it difficult for management to isolate causal factors in real time.

Disclosure Timing as Risk Signal

Several patterns in disclosure timing merit attention. The Medicaid rate-acuity mismatch was initially expected to "even out through remainder of 2024 and early 2025" (Q2 2024) but by Q3 2025 carried a 12–18 month lag extending "well into 2026," with negative margins now expected through 2026 as a trough year. Provider coding intensity was initially attributed to cyber-related waivers and expected to "dampen" (Q2 2024) but proved "notably and persistently aggressive" (Q3 2024) and evolved into a broader service intensity phenomenon. MA medical cost trend was first separately quantified at the point of maximum embarrassment — when the initial 5% assumption had to be raised to 7.5% and then 10%. In each case, the initial characterization proved more optimistic than warranted, and subsequent disclosures revealed incrementally worse dynamics.
