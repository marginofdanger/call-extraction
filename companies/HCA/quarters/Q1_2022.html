Guidance Revision Drivers (Three Primary Areas)

  • 1. Labor Costs (~2/3 of total revision along with inflation)
  • Cost of labor was higher than anticipated in Q1, primarily due to utilization and cost of contract labor
  • Disruption of labor market and pressure on labor cost inflation will be slower to moderate than originally anticipated
  • Cost per FTE was ~1.5% higher than planned in Q1
  • Projected full-year impact of elevated labor costs: $400 million to $500 million above original plan
  • Also adjusting base wages to be responsive to market conditions
  • 2. COVID Acuity / Revenue (~1/3 of total revision)
  • Reduced acuity and revenue from Omicron COVID patients in Q1
  • COVID inpatient revenue per admission down ~15% YoY
  • ~$150 million less COVID revenue in Q1 vs. prior year
  • Lower COVID acuity factored into remaining guidance periods
  • Revenue guidance decline is "vast majority" due to lower Omicron variant acuity
  • 3. Inflationary Pressures (included in the ~2/3 cost bucket)
  • Increased inflationary pressures expected going forward
  • Specific areas: professional fees, energy procurement, cost of utilities, and other purchased services

Volume Assumptions (Unchanged from Original Guidance)

  • Full Year Admissions Growth: +2% to +3% (unchanged)
  • COVID as % of Total Admissions (remaining quarters): 3% to 5% (down from 10% in Q1)
  • No more significant COVID surges assumed for the rest of the year

Revenue

  • Total Revenues: grew +6.9% YoY vs. Q1 2021
  • Same-Facility Inpatient Revenues: grew +5.4% YoY
  • Same-Facility Outpatient Revenues: grew +10.6% YoY
  • Non-COVID Revenue per Admission: grew +2.4% YoY (driven by maintained acuity levels and slightly favorable payer mix)

Earnings

  • Diluted EPS (excl. gains on sales of facilities): $4.12 (down $0.02 from Q1 2021 of $4.14)

Volume Metrics (Same-Facility)

  • Same-Facility Admissions: +2% YoY vs. Q1 2021
  • COVID-19 Inpatients: ~49,000 in the quarter; ~10% of total admissions (consistent with prior year)
  • Non-COVID Admissions: +2.2% YoY (growth occurred in February and March)
  • Non-COVID Admissions in Feb/March Specifically: growing at 4.5% to 5% rates
  • Inpatient Surgeries: grew ~+1% YoY
  • Same-Facility Emergency Room Visits: grew +15% YoY
  • Same-Facility Outpatient Surgeries: grew nearly +7% YoY
  • Outpatient Cardiac-Related Procedures: grew ~+7% YoY
  • Acuity levels and payer mix: continued to be strong across inpatient business

Volume vs. 2019 Baseline

  • Admissions and Adjusted Admissions vs. 2019: still down ~3% vs. 2019
  • 2021 vs. 2019 Baseline: down ~1%
  • Uninsured Volumes vs. 2019: down 11% (same-store basis)
  • Surgeries vs. 2019: actually up over 2019 levels
  • ER Visits (Paying Categories) vs. 2019: slightly up; uninsured ER activity way down
  • Context: Significant shift of orthopedic total joint surgeries from inpatient to outpatient from 2019 to 2022 pressured admission counts

COVID-Specific Metrics

  • COVID Inpatient Revenue per Admission: down ~15% YoY vs. Q1 2021 (lower acuity/intensity with Omicron variant)
  • Estimated COVID Revenue Impact: ~$150 million less COVID revenue YoY vs. Q1 2021 due to lower Omicron acuity
  • Same-Facility COVID Admissions: slightly above Q1 2021

Cash Flow & Balance Sheet

  • Cash Flow from Operations: $1.345 billion (vs. $2.0 billion in Q1 2021)
  • Deferred Payroll Tax Payment: $344 million paid in Q1 (representing 50% of total amount deferred from 2020)
  • Capital Spending: $860 million (vs. $650 million in Q1 2021)
  • Share Repurchases: just over $2.1 billion in the quarter
  • Debt-to-Adjusted EBITDA Ratio: slightly below the low end of target range
  • Available Liquidity (end of quarter): just under $7.9 billion
  • Planned Bond Redemption: ~$2.6 billion to redeem 2023 bonds in Q2 2022

Texas Waiver Program (CMS Directed Payment)

  • CMS Approval: Directed payment portion of Texas Waiver Program approved during March 2022
  • Total Revenue Recognized (Sept 1, 2021 - March 31, 2022): $385 million
  • Total Provider Tax Assessments (Sept 1, 2021 - March 31, 2022): $160 million
  • Of which, Sept-Dec 2021 portion: ~$244 million of revenue and ~$90 million of provider tax assessments
  • Implied Q1 2022-only portion: ~$141 million revenue and ~$70 million provider tax assessments

Labor / Cost Metrics

  • Contract Labor as % of Nursing Hours (Q1 2022): ~11.6%
  • Contract Labor as % of Nursing Hours (Q4 2021): 11.4%
  • Pre-COVID Benchmark: ~9% to 10% of hours
  • 2019 Benchmark: approximately half of current contract labor levels
  • Contract Labor Cost per Hour (Q1 vs. Q4): down 5% sequentially; improved each month within Q1
  • Cost per FTE vs. Plan: ~1.5% higher than originally planned in Q1
  • Cost per FTE Trend: labor costs per FTE have been roughly flat for 3 consecutive quarters (Q3 2021, Q4 2021, Q1 2022)
  • Contract Labor Typical Duration: ~13-week contracts
  • Original Plan for Cost per FTE Growth: 3% to 3.5% range
  • Actual Cost per FTE: ~1.5% above that plan (implying ~4.5% to 5% actual)
  • Contract labor costs flow through: the SWB (Salaries, Wages, and Benefits) line (not other operating)
  • Other Operating Expense Impact: primarily influenced by provider tax assessments from Texas Waiver

Facilities / Network

  • Total Facilities / Sites of Care (end of Q1): ~2,500 in HCA Healthcare networks
  • YoY Increase in Sites of Care: +15% over prior year

Adjusted EBITDA Margin

  • Implied Guidance Margin (full year): ~19% to 20% range (per CFO commentary)
  • Previous Target (discussed Q4 call): 20% to 21% margin as sustainable level
  • Q1 2022 Commentary: Q1 margins were "uniquely pressured" due to elevated contract labor levels and costs

Managed Care / Payer Contract Intelligence

  • 2022 Contracts: Largely contracted for 2022; no change to revenue mix expected from new contract negotiations in 2022
  • 2023 Contracts: ~50% contracted for 2023
  • 2024 Contracts: ~30% contracted for 2024
  • Pricing Discussion: More discussions with payers who understand inflationary pressures; opportunities to get escalators more in line with current inflation in 2023 and 2024 contracts
  • Management Tone: "Optimistic that we can gain some escalators that are more in line with the inflationary pressures of today versus the inflationary pressures of the past"

Medicare IPPS Proposed Rule for FY 2023

  • Gross Medicare Rate Increase (Proposed): just under +2% (consistent with historical levels)
  • Net Impact After Sequestration Delays: may be closer to flat net-net all-in
  • Forward-Year Outlook: Wage index adjustments typically lag; 2021 and 2022 actual cost data will eventually flow into the wage index formula and influence reimbursement in future years

Labor Strategy & Resiliency Initiatives

  • 1. Staffing & Capacity
  • Investing in and enhancing employee recruitment and retention efforts
  • Increased investment in recruiter headcount "significantly"
  • Common retention strategies being deployed organization-wide
  • Enhancing capacity management through new case management models and technology solutions
  • Exploring new delivery models through care transformation initiatives
  • New support staff roles: patient care techs, patient safety attendants
  • Galen College of Nursing expansion: new schools in Austin TX, Nashville TN, parts of South Carolina with "record-level enrollment"
  • Post-acute discharge planning improvements using new technologies for predictive patient assessments at discharge
  • Case management organizational structure being aligned enterprise-wide
  • 2. Resiliency Programs (Ongoing)
  • Next generation of shared services advancing efficiencies
  • Consolidation and alignment of laboratory operations
  • Facility management improvements
  • Environmental and food and nutrition support area consolidation
  • 3. Benchmarking Initiative
  • Advancing capability to benchmark key performance metrics across the organization
  • Intended to identify variation and opportunity to deploy best practices
  • Focus areas: supply utilization, provider support costs, discretionary spending, and other cost areas

Capacity & Operational Notes

  • Labor challenges constrained capacity in parts of Q1, preventing delivery of hospital services to certain patients
  • By end of Q1, capacity constraints were largely overcome
  • Transfer centers were able to operate normally and move patients to proper settings by quarter end
  • Post-acute supply/demand dynamics (skilled nursing, other post-acute settings) can cause backup in discharges, affecting length of stay
  • Census jumped +8.5% from Q2 2021 to Q3 2021 (record census levels ever for the company), which forced heavy contract labor usage

Price Transparency Compliance

  • HCA believes it is compliant with CMS price transparency rules
  • CMS has validated compliance in certain circumstances
  • Continuing to refine presentations to meet CMS evolving interpretations
  • Acknowledged the rules are "tremendously complex" due to variations across commercial contracts and markets

Management Qualitative Commentary (Key Quotes)

  • On the Demand Environment: "We continue to believe that overall demand for health care remains strong in our markets across most categories with favorable population trends and other contributing factors that developed during the pandemic driving it."
  • On Labor Cost Trajectory: "We now believe improvement in our labor cost will be slower than originally anticipated. This factor primarily influenced our revised outlook for 2022."
  • Labor costs per FTE have been roughly flat for 3 consecutive quarters, which management views as both stabilization and opportunity to reduce contract labor reliance
  • Contract labor cost per hour was down 5% sequentially from Q4 and improved each month within Q1
  • On Q1 Margin Pressure: "The margins in the first quarter were clearly pressured... with somewhat unprecedented levels of cost on the labor side."
  • Management believes margin recovery is achievable over time through workforce alignment away from contract labor
  • On Seasonality for 2022: Expects more normal volume seasonality in 2022 vs. prior two years; cost seasonality expected to differ as labor costs should improve sequentially
  • On Competitive Positioning: No unusual competitive maneuvers observed; HCA benefits from competitors being local/single-market operators
  • On Confidence / Outlook: "Over the past few years, we have demonstrated an ability to adjust effectively to whatever our realities are, and I'm confident we will do it again."
