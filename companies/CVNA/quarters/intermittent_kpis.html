What the Disclosure Patterns Reveal

1. The Survival Phase (Q1 2022 – Q1 2023): Crisis Metrics Dominate

The intermittent KPIs from this period tell a story of existential risk management. Liquidity disclosures appeared every quarter as cash burned at alarming rates. Going concern questions surfaced from Q3 2022 through Q1 2023. Inventory reduction targets were set and tracked obsessively. Finance receivables ballooned to $1.6B on the balance sheet. The language on these calls was defensive: "ride out this storm," "not need to raise additional capital," and the direct question of whether the Garcia family would "give up control." Reconditioning costs had surged >$600/unit, and IRC capacity sat at 3x the actual run rate — a painful reminder of how badly the growth overshoot of 2021-2022 had misfired.

2. The Turnaround Phase (Q2 2023 – Q4 2023): Operational Metrics Take Center Stage

The narrative shifted dramatically once EBITDA turned positive in Q2 2023. Going concern questions vanished overnight. In their place, Carvana began disclosing operational efficiency metrics with increasing confidence: the $1.2B annualized SG&A reduction, the activity pairing rate climbing from 7% to nearly 50%, reconditioning costs falling $900/unit, and the debt exchange that removed >$1.2B in face value. The Three-Step Plan — introduced during the darkest quarter — was already at Step 2. Liquidity disclosures became less prominent because the trajectory had reversed. The company was still selling 75K-80K units per quarter, far below its 2022 peak, but the unit economics were transforming.

3. The Growth Phase (Q1 2024 – Q4 2025): Infrastructure and Ambition Metrics Emerge

As growth resumed (+16% in Q1 2024, accelerating to +50% by Q4 2024), the intermittent KPIs shifted again. ADESA integration sites went from 1 to 34. Same-day delivery expanded from 11 markets to 35% of the U.S. population. IRC capacity and real estate capacity disclosures emphasized the 3M-unit runway. Stockholders' equity swung from -$384M to +$1,773M. And in Q1 2025, a bold new long-term target appeared: 3 million units per year at 13.5% EBITDA margins within 5-10 years. By Q3 2025, self-service rates were disclosed for the first time: 30% of buyers and 60% of sellers completing transactions without human contact.

4. The Arc as a Whole

Carvana's intermittent KPIs trace one of the most dramatic turnarounds in public market history. The company went from analysts questioning its survival to generating $2.2B+ in annual EBITDA in less than three years. Each category of intermittent metric corresponds to a specific phase of the arc: liquidity and going concern were the metrics of survival; cost reduction and activity pairing were the metrics of transformation; ADESA integration and same-day delivery are the metrics of ambition. The fact that many of these disclosures appeared intermittently — rather than quarterly — reflects the rapid evolution of what mattered most at each stage. The company disclosed what was existentially relevant in each moment, and those moments changed fast.

5. What Stopped Being Disclosed

The most telling signal is which metrics disappeared. Going concern questions lasted exactly three quarters. The $2.0-$2.5B inventory target range was set in Q2 2022 and stopped being referenced once inventory normalized. The explicit $1.2B cost reduction figure stopped being updated once the campaign was complete. These discontinuations are not gaps — they are markers of problems solved. Carvana's disclosure pattern is itself a narrative of resolution: each crisis-era metric that stops appearing is evidence that the underlying issue has been addressed.
